What's actually going on — plain English
Thread 1 · Pre-gate actions & CMMs

Your paper is trying to prove that "Constraint Management Mechanisms" are worth doing — and Saskia wants the argument tighter.

Constraint costs are what NESO pays to manage the fact that the grid can't move power from where it's generated to where it's needed. These are projected to hit £7–8bn/year by 2030 if nothing changes. CMMs (Constraint Management Mechanisms) are market tools that could help reduce those costs by paying generators or flexible assets to alter their output around constraints.

Your paper — the PG-CM Recommendation — is arguing for a particular approach. Saskia is supportive but wants you to:

"Contextualise the volumes — how big is this relative to what already happens in the Balancing Mechanism? And how much of the constraint problem can CMMs actually address?"

The CBA (cost-benefit analysis) that Agnes is running is the evidence base. But there's an upcoming update to the baseline balancing cost projections that could shift the numbers — so the CBA might need to move too. Izzie's team is helping quality-assure it.

The BM Plexos model (used for the modelling) has known limitations — it doesn't capture transmission outages well. The group agreed that CMMs should focus on structural constraints Plexos can model, not unpredictable outage-driven ones.

Thread 2 · Gas sets the price

NESO needed a defensible number for Parliament. The group built one — carefully.

The ESNZ Select Committee was asking: "How often does gas set the electricity price?" This sounds simple but isn't — gas can be running without setting the price, and gas can set the price even when renewables are generating a lot.

You can't directly observe the marginal (price-setting) plant, so the team built a proxy. Anthony Belcher's approach: compare the day-ahead electricity price against the Short Run Marginal Cost (SRMC) of running a gas plant. If electricity price is above what it costs to run gas, gas is probably marginal. This gives ~80–85% of settlement periods, depending on year and assumptions.

The answer is roughly "most of the time" — but the framing matters more than the number.

Externally (e.g. Modo, LinkedIn analysis) there are claims of a downward trend. NESO's internal numbers show it's broadly flat. The agreed position: present it as flat with uncertainty, explain the methodology clearly, and don't claim it as definitive. An alternative methodology surfaced (ECIU's SAP-offset approach) but wasn't adopted.

Thread 3 · RNP Delivery Plan (external, dropped while you were away)

DESNZ published the blueprint for everything you're working on.

The RNP Delivery Plan landed on 21 April. It's the government's plan for how GB electricity market reforms will happen post-REMA (which concluded in July 2025, retaining national pricing). It covers three challenge areas: locational investment signals, balancing and dispatch reform, and constraint management.

Key points relevant to your work: constraint management is explicitly in scope; the plan acknowledges operational reforms can cut constraint costs quickly (LCP Delta analysis suggests £6.1bn → £2.3bn by 2030 with a combined package); and decisions on siting and investment levers are expected H2 2026. A consultation on siting and investment levers is open until 2 June.
